## The Story of Mary

I want to tell you about Mary. She's 72. She's been retired for 7 years.

Mary worked hard her whole life. She raised three kids as a single mom. She put them all through college. She did everything she was supposed to do.

She gets Social Security. About $1,800 a month.

When she first retired, she thought that would be enough.

She'd done the math. Her mortgage was paid off. Her car was paid off. She thought she could make it work.

But here's what Mary discovered: $1,800 a month doesn't go as far as it used to.

Her mortgage is paid off, which helps. But there's still property taxes. $3,000 a year. Homeowners insurance. $1,200 a year. Utilities. $2,400 a year. Groceries. $6,000 a year. Healthcare costs that keep rising. $4,800 a year.

That's $17,400 a year, just for the basics.

And then there are the things she wants to do. Visit her grandchildren who live three states away. Take a trip she's been dreaming about. Help her kids when they need it. Buy a new car when hers finally dies.

$1,800 a month doesn't cover it.

So Mary does what millions of retirees do: she digs into her savings.

Every month, she takes a little bit more out of her IRA. Just enough to make ends meet. Just enough to cover the gap. $500 here. $300 there. Just enough to get by.

But here's what keeps her up at night: what happens when the savings run out?

She has about $180,000 left in her IRA. At the rate she's going, that might last another 10 years. Maybe 12 if she's careful.

But she's 72. What happens when she's 84? Or 90?

What happens when the savings are gone, and Social Security still isn't enough?

That's the question that keeps her up at night.
