David thought he was too late.

He was 62 years old. He'd spent 35 years building his retirement savings. He'd followed all the conventional advice. He'd done everything right.

But when he sat down to calculate his actual retirement income—after taxes, after healthcare costs, after accounting for market volatility—the numbers didn't add up. He had $900,000 saved, but it wasn't enough. Not for the retirement he wanted. Not for the security he needed.

David thought he'd missed his chance. He thought it was too late to make a difference. He thought he'd have to work longer, save more, or accept less.

But here's what David didn't know: it's never too late to create a better plan. It's never too late to restructure your strategy. It's never too late to build a foundation that actually works.

## The Crisis

David came to us in crisis.

He'd run the numbers. He'd used the calculators. He'd talked to other advisors. And every time, the answer was the same: he didn't have enough. He'd need to work until 70. He'd need to save more. He'd need to accept a lower standard of living.

David was frustrated. He'd done everything he was supposed to do. He'd saved consistently. He'd invested wisely. He'd followed the plan. But the plan wasn't working.

The problem wasn't David. The problem was the plan.

David's plan assumed perfect market timing. It assumed taxes would be lower in retirement. It assumed healthcare costs would be manageable. It assumed everything would go according to plan.

But here's what David's plan didn't account for: sequence risk. Tax drag. Healthcare costs. Longevity risk. The things that actually break retirement plans.

David's plan was a house of cards. And he knew it.

When we sat down with David, we didn't start with products. We didn't start with strategies. We started with understanding.

We asked David: "What does retirement actually look like for you? What are your real expenses? What are your real goals? What are you really trying to achieve?"

David's answer surprised him. He wasn't trying to maximize returns. He wasn't trying to beat the market. He was trying to create security. He was trying to create certainty. He was trying to create a retirement he could count on.

That's when David realized: he'd been solving the wrong problem.

David had been focused on accumulation. But what he really needed was income. He'd been focused on growth. But what he really needed was protection. He'd been focused on returns. But what he really needed was control.

The realization changed everything.

## The Strategy

We didn't rebuild David's plan from scratch. We restructured it.

We took David's $900,000 and we divided it into three buckets:

**Bucket 1: Foundation** (about $400,000)
- Guaranteed income that covers basic expenses
- Protected from market volatility
- Tax-efficient growth
- This bucket creates the floor. The foundation. The security.

**Bucket 2: Growth** (about $300,000)
- Market-based investments for growth
- Higher risk, higher potential return
- This bucket creates the upside. The growth. The opportunity.

**Bucket 3: Flexibility** (about $200,000)
- Liquid assets for emergencies
- Healthcare costs
- Unexpected expenses
- This bucket creates the buffer. The safety net. The peace of mind.

The strategy wasn't complicated. It wasn't revolutionary. It was simple. It was structured. It was designed to work whether markets go up or down.
