## 1. ANDY'S LANGUAGE PATTERNS

### A. Normalization Language

**Pattern:** Andy consistently normalizes client situations to reduce shame and anxiety.

**Examples:**
- "Well that's that's usually how layoffs work" (Oct 1 - Roger)
- "I know that life happens" (Oct 1 - Roger)
- "This is the exact kind of situation that you should be willing to go..." (Oct 10 - Dustin)
- "You guys are not some kind of outlier, there's nothing wrong with you" (Oct 10 - Dustin)
- "Almost everybody I work with has had something like that happen" (Oct 10 - Dustin)

**Psychological Impact:** Reduces isolation, shame, and self-blame. Moves client from "I'm a failure" to "This is normal."

### B. Reframing Language

**Pattern:** Andy reframes problems as opportunities or expected situations.

**Examples:**
- "This is what it's supposed to be there for" (Oct 1 - Roger, about policy loans)

**Psychological Impact:** Transforms crisis into expected/planned-for situation. Moves client from "This is a disaster" to "This is why we planned."

## 2. METAPHORS AND ALLEGORIES

### A. Savings Account Metaphor

**Usage:** Reframes policy loans as savings account withdrawals.

**Example (Oct 10 - Dustin):**
> "I don't want you to think of it as a payback schedule, any more than you would say. Hey, we had 45,000 in a savings account, we drew it down when we had to change careers, but now we're going to build our savings account back up."

**Psychological Impact:** Removes shame and obligation. Policy loans become normal financial tool, not debt.

### B. "Hiccup" / "Speed Bump" Metaphor

**Usage:** Minimizes crisis while acknowledging difficulty.

**Example (Oct 10 - Dustin):**
> "As you guys get this. I'm just gonna call it a hiccup, a speed bump. I'm sure that for both of you, it feels very like, oh my God, we're about to make a Monumental shift... But I will take, I will say this over the Long Haul... Your numbers. This is a hiccup, it's a speed bump."

**Psychological Impact:** Reduces catastrophic thinking. Crisis becomes temporary setback.

### C. "Black Hole" vs. "Your Pocket" Metaphor

**Usage:** Contrasts traditional insurance with HSA/self-directed accounts.

**Example (Oct 12 - Peggy):**
> "I might as well just, you know, start building a little savings account instead of it going into the black hole and then restarting next year again."

**Psychological Impact:** Creates clear value proposition. Traditional = loss, Self-directed = accumulation.

### D. "Pressure Relief Valve" Metaphor

**Usage:** Describes policy as safety mechanism.

**Example (Oct 10 - Dustin):**
> "For now, the pressure relief valve that you're looking for needs to come from the policy. It's that's what it's there for. That's what it's designed for."

**Psychological Impact:** Policy becomes tool for relief, not burden. Moves from obligation to resource.

### E. "Bucket" / "Container" Metaphor

**Usage:** Describes accumulation and deployment of funds.

**Example (Oct 10 - Dustin):**
> "You guys at 60. Yeah, you come to the table with a bunch of outstanding policy loans, all the money that you would that you would have had extra at 60 years old. Now, goes into that policy loan. And it grows even faster because you booked these 20 years old."

**Psychological Impact:** Creates visual of accumulation. Future self has "bucket" ready to fill.

## 5. SALES PROCESS STATE TRANSITIONS

### Generic State Progression Model

Based on analysis, here's the generic state progression:

#### **State 1: Crisis/Problem Recognition**
**Client State:**
- Emotional: Fear, anxiety, shame, overwhelm

- Behavioral: Urgent, reactive, seeking help

**Andy's Approach:**
- Listen and acknowledge
- Normalize situation
- Reduce shame
- Create safety

**Key Phrases:**
- "I understand"
- "This is normal"
- "Life happens"
- "We built this for this"

#### **State 2: Information Gathering**
**Client State:**
- Emotional: Slightly calmer, still anxious
- Cognitive: Seeking clarity, asking questions
- Behavioral: Providing information, exploring options

**Andy's Approach:**
- Ask clarifying questions
- Gather facts
- Explore options
- Provide education

**Key Phrases:**
- "Let me pull up your policy"
- "What do you think about..."

- "Let's take a look"

**Client State:**

- "Here's the good news"

### B. TONE AND PACING ANALYSIS

#### Tone Characteristics

**1. Conversational, Not Formal**
- Uses contractions ("I'm", "you're", "we're")
- Casual language ("gosh", "holy cow", "dang")
- Personal anecdotes and humor

**2. Patient and Non-Judgmental**
- Never rushes client
- Allows client to work through their own processes
- "No worries" / "Take your time" / "That's okay"

**3. Enthusiastic but Grounded**
- Shows excitement about opportunities
- But balances with reality ("I don't want to freak you out")
- "This is awesome" but "let's look at the numbers"

**4. Empathetic and Validating**
- Acknowledges client feelings
- "I understand that concern"
- "That makes sense"
- "I get why you'd feel that way"

#### Pacing Patterns

**1. Information Chunking**
- Breaks complex information into small pieces
- Pauses for client to process
- "Let me show you this" → pause → "Now here's what happens"

**2. Question-Answer Rhythm**
- Asks question → waits for answer → responds
- Never talks over client
- Allows silence for thinking

**3. Visual Tool Integration**
- Uses screen sharing/forecast tools
- "Let me pull this up"
- "You can see here..."
- Creates shared visual experience

**4. Natural Digressions**
- Allows personal conversation
- "Did you know I'm going to Pittsburgh?"
- "I love your face, though" (humor)
- Builds relationship, not just transaction

### G. PACING AND RHYTHM PATTERNS

2. Pause for processing

4. Wait for response
5. Proceed or clarify

> 
> Client: "right"
> 
> Andy: "When you stop working. I do have other strategies..."

**Why It Works:** Client processes information before next piece. Prevents overwhelm.

#### Pattern 2: **Question → Answer → Validation → Next**

**Structure:**
1. Ask question
2. Wait for answer
3. Validate answer ("Right", "Yeah", "Okay")
4. Ask next question or provide information

> Andy: "Yeah."
> 
> Client: "so I think okay so my tsp. I moved into those two annuities. And I think I left about $10,000."
> 
> Andy: "That's right. That's what we need."

**Why It Works:** Creates conversational flow. Client feels heard and validated.

#### Pattern 3: **Problem → Reframe → Solution → Future**

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge problem/concern
2. Reframe as opportunity/expected
3. Provide solution
4. Paint future vision

**Example:**
> Client: "I have an embarrassingly low amount of money for retirement"

## 12. FRICTION POINT ANALYSIS: IDENTIFYING AND RESOLVING CONFUSION AND OVERWHELM

### A. Types of Friction Points Identified

#### 1. **Large Number Overwhelm**

**Pattern:** Clients become overwhelmed when seeing large numbers (millions in taxes, millions in assets at age 101, etc.)

**Examples:**
- **Miranda Wood:** "Why are we in the millions? What the heck is going on?" (about tax projections)
- **Miranda Wood:** "I mean the 3 million is very nice but it's at 101. So that's like or else not I'm sorry I love you but I don't think you're going to live to average white 77 statistically."
- **Miranda Wood:** "That's so sad. That is very sad" (about running out of money at age 55-59)

**Andy's Resolution Techniques:**
1. **Immediate Contextualization:**
   - "usually what happens is this farther out you go, the less realistic it is. Partially because somewhere in here they're going to change the tax code."
   - "let me switch then and we'll do the live view so that you can see kind of apples to apples on the age what that looks like."

2. **Reframing as "Worst Case":**
   - "This is a worst case scenario"
   - "This is if your counseling business never grows beyond 3% per year"
   - "I'm trying to make it as hard as possible for myself"

3. **Breaking Down to Monthly/Daily:**
   - "That's almost two grand. Two grand a month that you guys are potentially going to be charged."
   - "It's roughly costing you $2.36 a day to have that student loan."

**Psychological Impact:** Transforms abstract large numbers into manageable, contextualized information.

---

#### 2. **Complexity/Technical Confusion**

**Pattern:** Clients express confusion about financial concepts, tax rules, or processes.

**Examples:**
- **Miranda Wood:** "Taxes are confusing. So"
- **Miranda Wood:** "I didn't know what is that like" (about Social Security)
- **Miranda Wood:** "It was very confusing. My grandma did our taxes"
- **Cheri M.:** "What's the purpose of putting the 200 to the policy and taking it right back out?"
- **Cheri M.:** "once you put it in, how long do you have to leave it in before you can pull it back out?"

**Andy's Resolution Techniques:**
1. **Simplification with Metaphors:**
   - "The policy is like a sailboat" (Elizabeth Wiltsie)
   - "I want you to think of the life insurance policy as your warehouse of wealth" (Cheri M.)
   - "It's like a savings account" (multiple clients)

2. **Step-by-Step Breakdown:**
   - "So, really, it's like I look at those regulations. Those are the guide book. What do you need to do in your life to make sure that something that income that you bring in is not taxable?"
   - "Here's how I'm going to set this up. First off, I want the the baseline to be as low as we possibly can get it."

3. **Validation of Confusion:**
   - "Yeah, there. Yeah, There's a lot to it, but I'll help you simplify it."
   - "I don't expect you would have known this"
   - "That's a loaded question, but part of it is I want to understand"

4. **Concrete Examples:**
   - "How long does it take a check to clear the bank? Three days. Five days. Yeah. So, five day. Let's just say five days."
   - "I had a woman who graduated from veterinary school... Her payments were $3,200 a month."

**Psychological Impact:** Reduces cognitive load. Client feels supported, not stupid.

**Friction Resolved:** Confusion about which debt to pay first and how to prioritize.

### F. PSYCHOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES FOR FRICTION RESOLUTION

#### Principle 1: **Never Leave Client in Confusion**

**Pattern:** Andy never moves forward if client is confused. Always resolves before proceeding.

**Example:**
> Client: "What's the purpose of putting the 200 to the policy and taking it right back out?"
> 
> Andy: "Because when you when you put it in there and you put it through that gate, that gate closes and it credits $200 as dividend eligible... So, $200 a month at 5% growing that up over a oh, I don't know, let's say a fiveyear period... That $200 a month turns into an additional $13,000"

**Why It Works:** Client feels heard and understood. Trust builds.

---

#### Principle 2: **Simplify, Don't Dumb Down**

**Pattern:** Andy uses sophisticated concepts but explains them simply. Never talks down to client.

**Example:**
> "The way that the way that that will depend on how we construct the policy. My goal will be to get it as close as possible that what you put in can come out. Now, there is a base amount that we're going to have to calculate and the total amount that you're allowed to put in depends on the original base amount that we set. So, it's a little bit like um have you ever played one of those games where you've got like maybe a grid of dots..."

**Why It Works:** Client feels respected. Concept is accessible without being condescending.

---

#### Principle 3: **Use Multiple Modalities**

**Pattern:** Andy uses words, visuals, examples, and metaphors to explain concepts.

**Example:**
- Explains concept verbally
- Shows visual tool/illustration
- Provides real-world example
- Uses metaphor for clarity

**Why It Works:** Different learning styles accommodated. Multiple reinforcement points.

---

#### Principle 4: **Celebrate Understanding**

**Pattern:** Andy acknowledges when client "gets it."

**Example:**
- "That makes sense, right?"
- "You got it!"
- "Exactly!"
- "Perfect!"

**Why It Works:** Positive reinforcement. Client feels successful.

### G. FRICTION POINT PREVENTION STRATEGIES

#### Strategy 1: **Proactive Simplification**

**Pattern:** Andy simplifies BEFORE client gets confused.

**Example:**
- "I don't want to freak you out because..." (before showing large numbers)
- "Let me break this down for you..." (before complex explanation)
- "Here's the simple version..." (before detailed explanation)

**Why It Works:** Prevents confusion rather than reacting to it.

---

#### Strategy 2: **Chunking Information**

**Pattern:** Information presented in small, digestible pieces.

**Example:**
- One concept at a time
- Pause for processing
- Check for understanding
- Then next concept

**Why It Works:** Prevents cognitive overload.

---

#### Strategy 3: **Visual Tools**

**Pattern:** Uses screen sharing, illustrations, calculators to show concepts.

**Example:**
- "Let me pull this up"
- "You can see here..."
- "Let me show you this"

**Why It Works:** Visual learners can "see" the concept. Reduces abstract thinking.

---

#### Strategy 4: **Real-World Examples**

**Pattern:** Uses client stories and case studies to illustrate concepts.

**Example:**
- "I had a woman who graduated from veterinary school..."
- "I've got people my oldest client is 97..."
- "I had a lady who went from 100,000 to 400,000..."

**Why It Works:** Makes abstract concepts concrete. Shows proof.

#### Non-Verbal Indicators (if visible):

- Looking away
- Fidgeting
- Long pauses
- Not responding
- Asking unrelated questions

#### Behavioral Indicators:
- Changing subject
- Expressing frustration
- Asking to "come back to that"
- Saying "I'll figure it out later"

**Andy's Response:** When these indicators appear, Andy immediately:
1. Stops current explanation
2. Acknowledges the friction
3. Simplifies or clarifies
4. Checks for understanding
5. Only proceeds when confirmed
